üåü A Story About Seeing Problems

It's 2008. Three friends are stuck in Paris because they can't afford hotel rooms. Instead of just complaining, they notice something deeper - thousands of people have empty spare rooms, while travelers need affordable places to stay. They don't just see their immediate problem; they see an unstated need across millions of people.

That observation became Airbnb, now worth $75 billion.

The difference between a regular person and a Product Manager? PMs see problems as opportunities, not obstacles. They dig deeper, ask "why" multiple times, and uncover needs people didn't even know they had.

üîç Two Paths to Problem Discovery

As a Product Manager, you'll discover problems through two main pathways. Understanding both is crucial for your success in the field and will definitely come up in your PM interviews.

Path 1: Problems Seen or Experienced Firsthand üëÅÔ∏è

This is where you observe daily life around you - your frustrations, your family's struggles, society's pain points.

Real Examples:

  • WhatsApp (2009): Brian Acton couldn't send photos reliably via SMS while traveling. He experienced the problem personally.
  • Paytm (2010): Vijay Shekhar Sharma struggled with mobile recharges and bill payments - a very personal Indian problem.
  • Zomato (2008): Deepinder Goyal and Pankaj Chaddah were tired of walking down office floors to check lunch menus.
  • Urban Company: Founders personally experienced the pain of finding reliable home services in Indian cities.

Path 2: Problems Pointed by Data üìä

For existing products, data reveals problems users might not explicitly state. You analyze user behavior, drop-off points, complaints, and usage patterns.

Real Examples:

  • Instagram Stories (2016): Data showed users were leaving Instagram to use Snapchat. The drop wasn't obvious until they analyzed user session data.
  • Netflix Autoplay: Data revealed 70% of users took more than 90 seconds to choose what to watch - a hidden friction point.
  • Swiggy's Live Tracking: Customer support data showed 40% of calls were "Where is my order?" - indicating an unstated need for transparency.
  • PhonePe QR Code: Data showed users were manually typing UPI IDs, creating errors and friction.

ü§î Think About This

Question for you: Look at your smartphone right now. What app did you use last? What small frustration did you encounter that you just accepted as "normal"? That's a potential product opportunity waiting to be explored.

üë©‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è Becoming a Problem Doctor

Great Product Managers are like doctors for problems. They don't just treat symptoms; they diagnose root causes. Here's how to develop this critical skill:

1. Deep Empathy with Users üíù

Empathy means feeling what your users feel, not just understanding what they say.

How to Practice:

  • Use your product like your most frustrated user would
  • Spend time with people outside your demographic
  • Watch users navigate your product without helping them
  • Read customer support tickets like stories, not data points

Example: Flipkart's Cash on Delivery

In 2010, Flipkart founders could have dismissed users saying "I don't trust online payments." Instead, they empathized deeply. They realized it wasn't about trust - it was about financial control and certainty in a country where most people didn't have credit cards. This empathy led to Cash on Delivery, which revolutionized Indian e-commerce.

2. Uncovering Unstated Needs üïµÔ∏è

Users often can't articulate their deepest needs. Henry Ford said, "If I asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses." Your job is to hear "faster horses" and understand they need "faster transportation."

üó£Ô∏è Stated vs Unstated Needs: The Hidden Goldmine

Understanding the difference between what users say they want (stated needs) and what they actually need (unstated needs) is crucial for product success. Most breakthrough products solve unstated needs.

Stated Needs - What Users Actually Say

These are explicit problems users can clearly articulate and are aware of.

Examples:

  1. "I want to book a cab faster" - Led to Ola/Uber's one-tap booking
  2. "I want to pay bills online" - Led to Paytm's bill payment feature
  3. "I want to order food without calling" - Led to Swiggy's app-based ordering
  4. "I want to watch movies at home" - Led to Netflix's streaming service
  5. "I want to send money to friends easily" - Led to UPI and digital wallets
  6. "I want to learn skills online" - Led to platforms like Coursera, Unacademy
  7. "I want to find restaurants nearby" - Led to Zomato's restaurant discovery

Unstated Needs - What Users Don't Even Know They Want

These are deeper, emotional, or unconscious needs that users haven't explicitly expressed but deeply feel.

Examples:

  1. Need for social validation - Instagram Likes weren't requested by users, but fulfilled a deep human need for approval
  2. Need for effortless decision-making - Netflix's "Because you watched..." wasn't asked for, but solved decision paralysis
  3. Need for financial transparency - Paytm's transaction history and spending insights weren't requested but provided financial awareness
  4. Need for safety assurance - Ola/Uber's driver photos and live tracking weren't explicitly demanded but addressed safety anxiety
  5. Need for social connection during transactions - WhatsApp Pay's integration wasn't requested but made payments feel more personal
  6. Need for progress visualization - Duolingo's streaks and progress bars weren't demanded but fulfilled the need to see growth
  7. Need for belonging and status - LinkedIn's endorsements and skill badges weren't requested but satisfied professional recognition needs

üéØ How to Identify Unstated Needs

Techniques to Uncover Hidden Needs:

  • Watch, don't just listen: Observe user behavior, not just their words
  • Ask "Why" 5 times: Dig deeper into motivations
  • Look for workarounds: What hacks are users creating?
  • Study emotional reactions: What makes users frustrated, delighted, or anxious?
  • Analyze the context: When, where, and why do users need your product?
  • Find the job behind the job: What is the user ultimately trying to achieve?

üìù Practice Exercise: Unstated Needs Detection

Exercise 1: Next time you're in a long queue (bank, restaurant, DMV), observe people around you. What are they doing? What emotions do you see? What unstated needs can you identify?

Exercise 2: Pick any app you use daily. List 3 stated needs it solves. Now list 3 unstated needs it might be addressing. Example: WhatsApp - Stated: "Send messages free", Unstated: "Feel connected to loved ones instantly"

Exercise 3: Interview a family member about a daily frustration. Ask "why" 5 times. You'll be surprised what unstated needs emerge!

üîÑ From Symptoms to Root Causes

One of the most critical skills for PM interviews and actual PM work is distinguishing between symptoms (what you see) and root causes (why it's happening). Most failed products solve symptoms, not root causes.

Real Example: Dropouts in Online Learning

Symptom:

"90% of students don't complete online courses"

Common Assumptions (Often Wrong):

  • Students are lazy
  • Content is too difficult
  • Course is too long
  • Students don't have time

Data Collection:

  • User interviews: "I lose motivation after week 2"
  • Analytics: 60% drop-off happens after first assignment
  • Support tickets: "I don't know if I'm learning correctly"

Root Causes Discovered:

  • Lack of immediate feedback creates uncertainty
  • No peer interaction leads to isolation
  • Unclear progress indicators cause confusion
  • Content lacks practical application

Solution:

Instead of making courses shorter (symptom-based solution), platforms like Coursera added peer reviews, progress bars, practical projects, and community features (root cause solutions).

Questions to Distinguish Symptoms from Root Causes

  1. "Is this the problem or a result of the problem?"
  2. "If we solve this, will similar issues stop recurring?"
  3. "What would have to be true for this symptom to exist?"
  4. "Are we solving the user's surface request or their deeper need?"
  5. "Would fixing this eliminate the user's underlying frustration?"

üõ†Ô∏è Problem Definition Frameworks

These frameworks are essential for PM interviews and will help you structure your problem-solving approach professionally.

1. Problem Statement Framework

Purpose: Convert vague issues into clear, actionable, and measurable problem definitions.

Structure: [User/segment] is struggling with [problem] because [cause], which leads to [impact]

Examples:

Good: "Middle-class Indian families are struggling to save money systematically because they lack awareness of their spending patterns and have no easy way to set aside small amounts regularly, which leads to financial stress and inability to build emergency funds."

Bad: "People don't save money" (too vague, no context, no cause, no impact)

Another Example: "Small restaurant owners in tier-2 Indian cities are struggling to reach new customers online because they lack digital marketing knowledge and affordable tools, which leads to reduced revenue and business closure during competitive periods."

2. Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) Framework

Purpose: Understand what users are trying to accomplish rather than what product they use.

Structure: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

Examples:

Netflix: "When I'm tired after work, I want to watch something entertaining without thinking, so I can relax and unwind."

Uber: "When I'm running late for a meeting, I want reliable transportation immediately, so I can arrive on time and maintain my professional reputation."

Instagram: "When I experience something interesting, I want to share it with people who care about me, so I can feel connected and get social validation."

3. 5 Whys Framework

Purpose: Find the root cause of a surface-level problem by repeatedly asking "why."

Example: E-commerce Cart Abandonment

  1. Why do users abandon their shopping carts?
    Because they leave at the payment page.
  2. Why do they leave at the payment page?
    Because they're hesitant to enter credit card details.
  3. Why are they hesitant to enter credit card details?
    Because they don't trust the security of the website.
  4. Why don't they trust the security?
    Because there are no visible security badges or trust signals.
  5. Why are there no trust signals?
    Because we focused on design aesthetics over user confidence.

Root Cause: Lack of visible trust indicators, not a payment flow problem.

4. User Persona Definition

Purpose: Create a clear representation of your target user(s) based on research

What to Include:

  • Demographics: Age, location, income, education
  • Psychographics: Values, attitudes, motivations
  • Behavioral patterns: How they currently solve the problem
  • Pain points: Current frustrations and challenges
  • Goals: What success looks like for them
  • Context: When, where, why they need your solution

Example Persona: "Priya - The Busy Professional"

Demographics: 28, Mumbai, ‚Çπ8-12 LPA, MBA graduate

Quote: "I want to eat healthy but don't have time to cook or research nutrition"

Pain Points: Long work hours, ordering junk food, guilt about health

Goals: Stay healthy without spending too much time or money

Context: Orders food during office hours, uses phone during commute

üéØ Placement Tip

Interview Gold: When asked "How would you improve WhatsApp?", don't immediately suggest features. First, use these frameworks:

  1. Define user personas ("Working professionals in India")
  2. Use JTBD ("When I'm in a group chat with office colleagues, I want to communicate professionally, so I can maintain work relationships")
  3. Apply 5 Whys to identify root causes
  4. Create a problem statement

This structured approach shows PM thinking, not just feature brainstorming.

üí™ Practical Exercises

Exercise 1: Problem Observation Challenge

For the next 7 days, carry a small notebook. Write down 3 problems you observe each day - things that frustrate you, inefficiencies you notice, or needs you see in others. At the end of the week, categorize them as stated vs unstated needs.

Exercise 2: 5 Whys Practice

Pick a common complaint about any popular app (slow loading, confusing interface, etc.). Apply the 5 Whys framework. Compare your root cause with the actual features the company has released to address similar issues.

Exercise 3: JTBD Analysis

Choose 3 apps you use regularly. For each, write their JTBD statement. Then think: what job are they really being hired to do beyond their obvious function? (Example: Instagram isn't just for photo sharing - it's hired for social validation and entertainment)

Exercise 4: Data Detective

Find a public case study of any product pivot (Twitter to X, Instagram adding Stories, etc.). Identify what data or user behavior led to that change. What unstated need did they discover?

Exercise 5: Persona Building

Interview 3 people (family/friends) about their biggest daily frustration. Create personas for each. Practice the problem statement framework for their issues. This will help you in PM interviews when asked to analyze user problems.

Exercise 6: Symptom vs Root Cause

List 5 problems in your college/workplace. For each, identify if it's a symptom or root cause. For symptoms, dig deeper to find root causes. For root causes, think about what symptoms they might create.

Exercise 7: Framework Application

Take any current startup in India (pick from news/funding announcements). Apply all 4 frameworks to analyze their problem space. This type of analysis is commonly asked in PM interviews.

üéØ Placement Preparation Tips

How to Demonstrate Problem Identification Skills in Interviews

1. Structure Your Answers Using Frameworks

When asked "How would you solve X problem?", always start with:

  • "Let me first understand the user and their context..."
  • "I'd like to define the problem clearly using a problem statement..."
  • "Let me apply the 5 Whys to ensure we're solving the root cause..."

2. Show Empathy and User Focus

Always mention user emotions and context:

  • "Users feel frustrated when..."
  • "The emotional impact of this problem is..."
  • "From the user's perspective..."

3. Distinguish Between Stated and Unstated Needs

Impress interviewers by saying:

  • "While users say they want X, what they really need is Y"
  • "The stated need is clear, but I suspect there's an unstated need for..."

Common PM Interview Questions About Problem-Solving

1. "How would you improve [popular app]?"

Structure: User personas ‚Üí JTBD ‚Üí Problem identification ‚Üí 5 Whys ‚Üí Solution

2. "Design a product for [specific user group]"

Structure: User research ‚Üí Problem statement ‚Üí Unstated needs analysis ‚Üí Solution frameworks

3. "Why do you think [product] failed?"

Structure: Symptom analysis ‚Üí Root cause identification ‚Üí Market/user need mismatch

4. "How would you prioritize these problems?"

Structure: Impact vs effort ‚Üí User pain severity ‚Üí Business alignment ‚Üí Data validation

How to Structure Your Answers

The CIRCLES Method (adapted for problem identification):

  • Clarify the problem and context
  • Identify the user and their needs
  • Report the user's goals and motivations
  • Cut through assumptions with frameworks
  • List solutions based on root causes
  • Evaluate trade-offs
  • Summarize your recommendation
